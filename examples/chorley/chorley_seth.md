# Chorley & Seth (2011): a circuit that *generates* the dopamine reward-prediction signal

A reduced-scale reproduction of Chorley, P. & Seth, A. K. (2011), *“Dopamine-Signaled
Reward Predictions Generated by Competitive Excitation and Inhibition in a Spiking
Neural Network Model,”* Front. Comput. Neurosci. 5:21.

- Module: `snn2/chorley_seth.py`
- Example: `examples/chorley_seth.py`
- Run validation: `python -m snn2.chorley_seth`
- Figure: `python -c "from snn2.chorley_seth import make_figure; make_figure('chorley_seth_fig.png')"`

---

## 1. Why this one is different

Every other reproduction in snn2 (Florian, Farries & Fairhall) *consumes* a
reward / dopamine signal to drive learning. This model asks the upstream question:
**where does that dopamine reward-prediction signal come from?** Chorley & Seth give
it a dedicated circuit — and show that the canonical shift of the dopamine response
from the reward to the reward-predicting cue (Schultz’s midbrain recordings; the
hallmark of TD learning) *emerges* from competition between two pathways under
dopamine-modulated plasticity. It complements Izhikevich (2007), who noted the same
shift arose spontaneously but modeled only that one feature.

---

## 2. The circuit

Two parallel pathways carry a sensory event to the dopamine (DA) neurons:

```
fast / excitatory :  SEN ──▶ INT ──▶ DA          (no latency; a fast relay, e.g. STN/SC)
slow / inhibitory :  PFC ──▶ STR ──┤ DA          (100 ms cortical latency)
```

- **SEN** (sensory), split into US- and CS-specific halves.
- **INT** (intermediate excitatory relay), parallel-wired from SEN, **excites** DA.
- **PFC** (prefrontal cortex), which responds to a stimulus with a *sustained 1 s,
  stimulus-specific, polychronous* spike pattern (no firing-rate change — only
  timing carries the stimulus identity).
- **STR** (striatum), sparsely wired from PFC, **inhibits** DA.
- **DA** (dopamine neurons), whose output is a *race* between fast INT excitation
  and delayed STR inhibition.

Both **SEN→INT** and **PFC→STR** are plastic. The DA output therefore controls, and
is controlled by, a precisely timed balance of excitation and inhibition.

All neurons are Izhikevich (2003) regular-spiking units. Two control signals close
the loop:

- **DA-STDP.** STDP builds a synaptic eligibility trace `γ`; the weight changes as
  `dω/dt = α·γ`, where `α` is the extracellular dopamine level (rises with DA
  firing, decays with `τα = 0.1 s`). This is Izhikevich’s distal-reward mechanism:
  an eligibility tag laid down by spike timing is later cashed in by a dopamine
  burst.
- **DA-PSF** (post-synaptic facilitation). Dopamine also raises STR *excitability*
  (it modulates the Izhikevich `b` parameter), so a DA burst makes STR neurons fire
  just afterward — tagging the PFC inputs active around that time for potentiation.

## 3. How the shift is supposed to work

The protocol: 100 trials, each CS then US 500 ms later. The US is wired as intrinsic
reward — US-SEN→US-INT synapses start at maximum, so the US drives a strong DA burst
through the fast pathway. Everything else starts silent.

- **Cue pathway (SEN→INT).** When the CS fires SEN, background activity makes some
  INT-CS neurons fire shortly after, laying an eligibility trace on CS-SEN→CS-INT.
  500 ms later the US-driven DA burst raises `α` and converts that trace into a
  weight increase (the SEN eligibility time-constant is 1 s, long enough to bridge
  the gap). Trial by trial the cue pathway strengthens until **the CS itself drives
  a DA response** — the dopamine response appears at the cue.
- **Inhibitory pathway (PFC→STR).** The sustained CS-specific PFC pattern covers the
  US time. DA-PSF makes STR fire around the US-driven DA burst; the eligibility trace
  (PFC time-constant 0.2 s) lets STR firing in the window *before* the burst be tagged
  and potentiated. STR thus learns to fire a precisely timed volley at US time that
  **cancels** the (still-present) INT excitation — suppressing the DA response to the
  now-predicted US.

Net effect: the DA response moves from the US to the CS.

## 4. What this reproduction shows (and its honest limits)

`snn2/chorley_seth.py:validate()` runs 100 trials and checks (all pass):

1. **Before training, DA responds to the US, not the CS** (US > 2× CS).
2. **A DA response to the CS emerges with training** (post-CS ≫ pre-CS).
3. **After training DA responds strongly to the CS** (post-CS > 2× pre-CS).
4. **The inhibitory pathway develops anticipatory STR firing near the US**
   (STR firing in the pre-US window grows markedly over training).
5. **The US response is suppressed relative to before** (post-US < pre-US).
6. **Omitting the CS does not reduce the US response** (the unexpected-reward
   sensitivity: with no CS there is no anticipatory inhibition).

The figure (`chorley_seth_fig.png`) shows the CS response climbing from ~9 to ~30
spikes, the peri-event histogram going from a single US peak (before) to **both a CS
and a US peak** (after), and the anticipatory STR firing developing over trials.

### The honest limit: full US *cancellation* is scale-limited

The **cue-response emergence** (the dopamine response appearing at the CS) is robust
here. The **complete cancellation** of the US response — Chorley & Seth’s distinctive
addition over Izhikevich — is only **partial** at this reduced scale. The reason is
specific and worth stating: cancelling a sharp US-driven DA burst (≈20% of DA neurons
firing in the same millisecond) requires an equally sharp, strong, *synchronized*
inhibitory volley from STR at exactly that millisecond. That precision comes from the
paper’s large polychronous substrate — 100 STR neurons each receiving 100 afferents
from 1000 PFC neurons with rich, repeatable fine-timing structure. At tens of neurons
with random patterns, STR develops the right *anticipatory* firing (panel C) but it is
too diffuse to fully null the burst, so suppression is modest. This is a property of
the reduced scale, not of the mechanism — and it is exactly the kind of thing the
snn2 validation methodology is meant to make explicit rather than paper over.

The two not-yet-reproduced paper results that depend on full cancellation — the
sharp recovery to *unexpected* US (Fig 3) and the below-baseline **dip** on reward
omission (Fig 6) — would follow once the inhibitory volley is sharp enough; they are
listed as extensions below.

## 5. Parameters

From the paper (`snn2/chorley_seth.py:P`), with group sizes and per-neuron afferent
counts reduced for tractability, and the fixed INT→DA / STR→DA weights rescaled to
compensate for the smaller afferent count (effective drive ∝ afferents × weight).

| field | value | paper | role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Izhikevich RS | a .02 b .2 c −65 d 8 | same | all neurons |
| `A_plus / A_minus` | 0.1 / 0.15 | same | STDP up/down |
| `tau_stdp` | 20 ms | 20 ms | STDP window |
| `tau_gamma_sen / _pfc` | 1000 / 80 ms | 1000 / 200 ms | eligibility decay (PFC sharpened for timing at small scale) |
| `tau_alpha` | 100 ms | 100 ms | dopamine decay |
| `da_step` | 0.05 | 0.05 | α increment per DA spike |
| `w_max` | 10 | 10 | plastic ceiling |
| `n_sen/int/pfc/str/da` | 40/40/120/40/40 | 100/100/1000/100/100 | reduced sizes |
| `aff` | 12 | 100 | afferents per neuron (reduced) |
| `w_int_da / w_str_da` | 3 / 14 | 0.6 / 1 | rescaled for reduced afferents |
| `lr` | 0.2 | (units) | scales `dω = lr·α·γ` |

## 6. How to run / extend

```bash
python -m snn2.chorley_seth        # 6 validation checks + summary
python examples/chorley_seth.py    # also renders chorley_seth_fig.png
```

- **Sharpen the inhibitory volley** (toward full cancellation, Fig 4C): scale the
  network up (more STR/PFC neurons, more afferents) so STR can fire a synchronized
  millisecond-precise volley; and/or give PFC explicit polychronous groups rather
  than i.i.d. random currents, so specific PFC ensembles reliably co-fire just
  before the US.
- **Unexpected-reward recovery (Fig 3).** With full cancellation in place, present
  the US alone on trial 101: no CS → no PFC pattern → no anticipatory STR inhibition
  → the US response returns at full strength.
- **Reward-omission dip (Fig 6).** Present the CS alone: STR still fires its learned
  inhibitory volley at the expected US time, but now there is no INT excitation to
  cancel, producing a precisely timed *below-baseline* dip — a negative
  prediction error.
